Converting from rectangular to polar

Since the rectangular and polar coordinate systems are descriptions of the same underlying plane, we can superimpose the two systems on each other. In doing so, we let the rectangular system origin and the polar system pole be the same point. We let the polar system polar axis be the same as the right, or positive, half of the \(x\)-axis. To convert from rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\) to polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\), we use the Pythagorean theorem, \(r^2=x^2+y^2,\) and the definition of tangent, \(\tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}.\) Be careful to use your knowledge about the quadrant of your point to help you compute the correct angle.

Example: converting from rectangular to polar coordinates

Convert \((-3,4)\) to polar coordinates.

We can compute \(r\) using \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\). Thus \(r=\sqrt{9+16}=5\). To find the angle \(\theta\), we use \(\tan\theta=-\frac{4}{3}\). Notice that \((-3,4)\) is in Quadrant II, and so our answer for \(\theta\) must be a second quadrant angle. So \(\theta=180^\circ+\tan^{-1}(-4/3)\approx 127^\circ\).

Thus the point with rectangular coordinates \((-3,4)\) can be represented approximately by \((5,127^\circ)\) in polar coordinates.

Converting from polar to rectangular

Similarly, we can use what we already know to convert from polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\) to rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\).

To convert from polar to rectangular, we use the definitions of sine and cosine to write the equations \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\). These formulas are true even when \(r\) is negative because of the way we deal with negative \(r\) values!

If you want a more detailed explanation of why these formulas work when \(r\) is negative, consider the fact that \((r,\theta)=(-r,\theta+180^\circ)\). Assume \(r\) is negative. Then \(-r\) is positive. So we can convert to rectangular: \(x=-r\cos(\theta+180^\circ)=-r(-\cos\theta)=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=-r\sin(\theta+180^\circ)=-r(-\sin\theta)=r\sin\theta\), where \(\cos(\theta+180^\circ)=-\cos\theta\) and \(\sin(\theta+180^\circ)=-\sin\theta\) by the sum identities from trigonometry. So regardless of whether \(r\) is positive or negative, the formulas \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\) work to convert from polar to rectangular.

Example: converting from polar to rectangular

Convert \((12,-45^\circ)\) to rectangular coordinates.

We can compute \(x\) using \(r\cos\theta\) and \(y\) using \(r\sin\theta\). Thus \(x=12\cdot\cos(-45^\circ)=12\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}=6\sqrt{2}\) and \(y=12\cdot\sin (-45^\circ)=12\cdot-\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}=-6\sqrt{2}\)

Thus the point with polar coordinates \((12,-45^\circ)\) can be represented approximately by \((6\sqrt{2},-6\sqrt{2})\) in rectangular coordinates.

To summarize, we use the Pythagorean theorem and the definition of the trig functions to convert between polar and rectangular coordinates:

Conversion Equations Between Polar and Rectangular Coordinates

When converting from polar \((r,\theta)\) to rectangular \((x,y)\), use \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\).

When converting from rectangular \((x,y)\) to polar \((r,\theta)\), use \(x^2+y^2=r^2\) and \(\tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}\). Take care to make sure your angle is in the correct Quadrant.
