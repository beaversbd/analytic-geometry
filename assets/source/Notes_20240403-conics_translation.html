So far we have centered all of our conic sections at \((0,0)\), but we don't want that to always be the case. We want to be able to have conic sections that are shifted away from the origin.

As a simple example, suppose we wanted to take the unit circle \(x^2+y^2=1\) and shift all the points one unit to the right. The point \((1,0)\) moves to \((2,0)\), the point \((-1,0)\) moves to \((0,0)\), the point \((0,1)\). In this scenario, a point \((x',y')\) is on the graph of the unit circle \((x')^2+(y')^2=1\) if and only if the point \((x'+1,y')=(x,y)\) is on the shifted graph. Thus \(y'=y\) and \(x-1=x'\). As a consequence, we get the equation \((x-1)^2+y^2=1\). So the point \((2,0)\) satisfies \((x-1)^2+y^2=1\), and it corresponds to the point \((1,0)\) on the unit circle \((x')^2+(y')^2=1\). In other words, to get a unit circle point from a shifted point, all we need do is subtract one from the \(x\)-coordinate of the point; this is what gives us the shifted equation \((x-1)^2+y^2=1\).

A similar process allows us to shift in a vertical direction: replacing \(y\) in an equation by \(y-2\) shifts the points up two units. Thus the equation for the circle obtained by shifting the unit circle up \(2\) units is \(x^2+(y-2)^2=r^2\).

We can combine the shifts (right one and up two) to get yet another equation, \((x-1)^2+(y-2)^2=1\).

In general, we can shift the graph of an equation by \(h\) units right (left if \(h\) is negative) and \(k\) units up (down if \(k\) is negative) by replacing \(x\) with \(x-h\) and \(y\) by \(y-k\) in the equation.

For circles, shifting does not affect the radius, but it does move the center to \((h,k)\):

Standard Form Equation for a Circle Centered at \((h,k)\)

The standard form equation for a circle centered at \((h,k)\) is \[(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2=r^2.\]

Key information:

  • center: \((h,k)\)
  • radius: \(r\)

Let's see how shifting affects the key information of the rest of our conic sections.

Standard Form Equation for an Ellipse with Center \((h,k)\) and Horizontal Major Axis

The standard form equation for an ellipse with center \((h,k)\) and horizontal major axis is \[\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}+\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(a^2=b^2+c^2\).

Key information:

  • center: \((h,k)\)
  • vertices: \((h\pm a,k)\)
  • covertices: \((h,k\pm b)\)
  • foci: \((h\pm c,k)\)
  • major axis: \(y=k\)
  • minor axis: \(x=h\)

Standard Form Equation for an Ellipse with Center \((h,k)\) and Vertical Major Axis

The standard form equation for an ellipse with center \((h,k)\) and vertical major axis is \[\frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2}+\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(a^2=b^2+c^2\).

Key information:

  • center: \((h,k)\)
  • vertices: \((h,k\pm a)\)
  • covertices: \((h\pm b,k)\)
  • foci: \((h,k\pm c)\)
  • major axis: \(x=h\)
  • minor axis: \(y=k\)

Standard Form Equation for a Parabola with Vertex \((h,k)\) Opening Upward

The standard form equation for a parabola vertex \((h,k)\) opening upward is \[(x-h)^2=4c(y-k).\]

Key information:

  • vertex: \((h,k)\)
  • focus: \((h,k+c)\)
  • directrix: \(y=k-c\)
  • axis of symmetry: \(x=h\)

Standard Form Equation for a Parabola with Vertex \((h,k)\) Opening Downward

The standard form equation for a parabola vertex \((h,k)\) opening downward is \[(x-h)^2=-4c(y-k).\]

Key information:

  • vertex: \((h,k)\)
  • focus: \((h,k-c)\)
  • directrix: \(y=k+c\)
  • axis of symmetry: \(x=h\)

Standard Form Equation for a Parabola with Vertex \((h,k)\) Opening Rightward

The standard form equation for a parabola vertex \((h,k)\) opening rightward is \[(y-k)^2=4c(x-h).\]

Key information:

  • vertex: \((h,k)\)
  • focus: \((h+c,k)\)
  • directrix: \(x=h-c\)
  • axis of symmetry: \(y=k\)

Standard Form Equation for a Parabola with Vertex \((h,k)\) Opening Leftward

The standard form equation for a parabola vertex \((h,k)\) opening leftward is \[(y-k)^2=-4c(x-h).\]

Key information:

  • vertex: \((h,k)\)
  • focus: \((h-c,k)\)
  • directrix: \(x=h+c\)
  • axis of symmetry: \(y=k\)

Standard Form Equation for a Hyperbola with Center \((h,k)\) and Horizontal Transverse Axis

The standard form equation for a hyperbola with vertex \((h,k)\) and horizontal transverse axis is \[\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2}-\frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Key information:

  • center: \((h,k)\)
  • vertices: \((h\pm a,k)\)
  • foci: \((h\pm c,k)\)
  • transverse axis: \(y=k\)
  • conjugate axis: \(x=h\)
  • asymptotes: \(y-k=\pm\frac{b}{a}(x-h)\)

Standard Form Equation for a Hyperbola with Center \((h,k)\) and Vertical Transverse Axis

The standard form equation for a hyperbola with vertex \((h,k)\) and vertical transverse axis is \[\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2}-\frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Key information:

  • center: \((h,k)\)
  • vertices: \((h,k\pm a)\)
  • foci: \((h,k\pm c)\)
  • transverse axis: \(x=h\)
  • conjugate axis: \(y=k\)
  • asymptotes: \(y-k=\pm\frac{a}{b}(x-h)\)
