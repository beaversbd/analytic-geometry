Hyperbolas

The final conic sections we will consider are the hyperbolas. It is defined using a tweak on the the definition for ellipses. With ellipses, the sum of the distances from a point on the ellipse to the two foci is a constant \(2a\). For hyperbolas, we will have two foci, but a point will be on the hyperbola if the (postive) difference of the distances from the point to the foci is a constant \(2a\).

The end result is a shape divided into two branches, each of which contains a focus.

We can use a similar derivation as with ellipses. Suppose the foci are at \((c,0)\) and \((-c,0)\), and that the constant difference of the distances is \(2a.\) Then, \[ \begin{align} \sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2} - \sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} & = \pm 2a \\ \sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2} & = \sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} \pm 2a \\ x^2-2cx+c^2+y^2 & = x^2+2cx+c^2+y^2 \pm 4a\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2}+4a^2 \\ \mp 4a\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} & = 4a^2+4cx \\ \mp \sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} & = a+\frac{cx}{a} \\ x^2+2cx+c^2+y^2 & = a^2+2cx+\frac{c^2x^2}{a^2} \\ \frac{c^2-a^2}{a^2}x^2-y^2 & = c^2-a^2 \\ \frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{c^2-a^2} & = 1. \end{align} \]

Finally, let \(b^2=c^2-a^2\) (we'll see how this relates to the graph in a little while) to get \[\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1.\]

As with an ellipse, the points \((a,0)\) and \((-a,0)\) satisfy the equation. Thus, if we want a true hyperbola, \(c\) needs to be larger than \(a\). Thus \(c^2>a^2\),and so \(c^2-a^2\) is positive. Thus \(b^2\) must also be positive. Since we defined \(b^2\) as \(c^2-a^2\), we can rearrange the equation \(c^2-a^2=b^2\) to get \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Now that we have an equation, let's review the key points on a hyperbola:

We already have seen the foci \((c,0)\) and \((-c,0)\). We also have the points \((a,0)\) and \((-a,0)\) where the hyperbola crosses the \(x\)-axis; we call these points the vertices of the hyperbola. The \(x\)-axis, as the line that contains the vertices and foci, is called the transverse axis of the hyperbola. The center of the hyperbola (in this case, \((0,0)\)) is the midpoint between the foci on the transverse axis.

The \(y\)-axis, being perpendicular to the transverse axis, is called the conjugate axis. The center is also the point of intersection of the transverse axis with the conjugate axis.

For extremely large values of \(x\) and \(y\), the terms \(\frac{x^2}{a^2}\) and \(\frac{y^2}{b^2}\) are significantly larger than \(1\), so much so, in fact, that they are comparatively equal: \(\frac{x^2}{a^2}\approx\frac{y^2}{b^2}.\) Thus the further away from the origin we go, the closer the hyperbola begins to resemble the lines \(y=\frac{b}{a}x\) and \(y=\frac{b}{a}x\). We say that these two lines are the asymptotes of the hyperbola (dotted lines in the graph above).

To summarize, the important points and lines of a hyperbola are the following:

  • foci
  • transverse axis
  • center
  • vertices
  • conjugate axis
  • asymptotes

Now we can summarize all this information:

Standard Form Equation for a Hyperbola with Foci \((\pm c,0)\) on the \(x\)-axis

The standard form equation for a hyperbola with foci \((\pm c,0)\) on the \(x\)-axis is \[\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Key information:

  • foci: \((\pm c,0)\)
  • transverse axis: \(y=0\)
  • center: \((0,0)\)
  • vertices: \((\pm a,0)\)
  • conjugate axis: \(x=0\)
  • asymptotes: \(y=\pm\frac{b}{a}x\)

What if we had started with the foci on the \(y\)-axis instead? Then everything we just did still holds, except that we interchange the roles of \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinates:

Standard Form Equation for a Hyperbola with Foci \((0,\pm c)\) on the \(y\)-axis

The standard form equation for a hyperbola with foci \((0,\pm c)\) on the \(y\)-axis is \[\frac{y^2}{a^2}-\frac{x^2}{b^2}=1,\] where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive numbers and \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Key information:

  • foci: \((0,\pm c)\)
  • transverse axis: \(x=0\)
  • center: \((0,0)\)
  • vertices: \((0,\pm a)\)
  • conjugate axis: \(y=0\)
  • asymptotes: \(y=\pm\frac{a}{b}x\)

The key to distinguishing between a "horizontal" hyperbola and a "vertical" hyperbola is which term is negative. As opposed to an ellipse where \(a^2\) is the larger denominator, in a hyperbola \(a^2\) is the denominator of the positive term. That is, \(a^2\) is always the denominator of the positive term, \(b^2\) is the denominator of the negative term, and \(c^2\) can be found from these by using the equation \(c^2=a^2+b^2\).

Sometimes a hyperbola is not given in standard form, and you will need to use algebra and manipulate the equation until it is in standard form before you can analyze and create a graph.
