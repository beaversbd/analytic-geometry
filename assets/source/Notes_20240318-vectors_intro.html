Definition of Vector

There are many things in real life that have more to them than just an amount. One natural example of this is the concept of velocity in physics. As an object moves, it has more than just a speed. This motion also also has a direction associated with the motion. You end up at a different place if you are driving at 50mph to the south versus. 50mph to the north. A box will move differently if you exert a force to one direction versus another. As a result of this need, we define a vector quantity (or just vector) as a quantity that has both a magnitude, a number part, and a direction. With the example of velocity, the number representing speed, as separate from the direction, is the magnitude of the vector. Two vectors are equal when have the same magnitude and direction.

Definition of Vector

A vector is a quantity that has both magnitude and direction.

The easiest way to conceive of a vector is to think of a vector as an arrow, or a line segment with a pointer on one end to indicate direction, as in the following picture:

A line segment drawn this way is also called a directed line segment. The length of the line segment - the length of the arrow from the tail to the tip - is considered to be the magnitude. It's a little harder to describe the direction. Often this direction is described by the angle the vector makes with respect to the horizontal. In the picture above, the arrow appears to be making an approximately \(45^\circ\) angle with the horizontal.

Generally, vectors are denoted by lower-case letters either with an arrow on top, like \(\overrightarrow{v},\) or in bold, like \(\mathbf{v}.\)

When our context includes vectors, then we distinguish between vector quantities and plain numbers. In this case, we call plain real numbers scalars.

This process of multiplying a vector by a real number, or scalar, is called scalar multiplication. Multiplying a vector by a number \(r\) yields a vector whose magnitude is \(|r|\) times that of the original. If \(r\) is negative, it also reverses the direction of the vector.

Defining the Components of a Vector

To make it easier to describing and compute with vectors, we often put the vector, like we do with angles, in a standard way. Every vector that we draw in the cartesian plane has a initial point (tail) and then we draw the vector toward its other end, the end with the arrow, called the terminal point (tip). Shifting the vector around the plane does not change the vector in any way, because it does not change the vector's length or direction. If we draw a vector in the cartesian plane with the initial point at \((0,0),\) we say the vector is in standard position, as in the picture below. In that picture, the point \(A\) is the initial point (at the origin), the point \(B\) is the terminal point, and the vector is the directed line segment.

It is often easier to describe a vector not by its magnitude and direction, but by breaking up the arrow into a combination of a horizontal amount plus a vertical amount, as shown in the picture below:

The direction of this vector is included in the idea that the vector is made up of two units of horizontal motion followed by three units of vertical motion. We say these two numbers are the components of the vector, and write it as \(\langle 2,3\rangle.\)

We can still find the components even if the vector is not in standard position. If the initial point of the vector is \((x_1, y_1)\) and the terminal point of the vector is \((x_2,y_2),\) then the components of vector are the horizontal component \(x_2-x_1\) and the vertical component \(y_2-y_1.\) Then the component form of the vector is \(\langle x_2-x_1,y_2-y_1\rangle.\)

The component form of vectors is much easier to use than the geometric forms we have been using. For instance, we can tell of two vectors are the same by comparing components. Assuming \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle\) and \(\overrightarrow{u}=\langle u_1,u_2\rangle,\) then \(\overrightarrow{v}=\overrightarrow{u}\) if, and only if, \(v_1=u_1\) and \(v_2=u_2.\)

How can we get the magnitude and direction from the components of a vector? By definition of components, the vector is the hypotenuse of a right triangle whose legs represent the components. Then the length of the hypotenuse can be found from the legs by using our old friend, the Pythagorean theorem!

Magnitude of a Vector

For a vector \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle,\) the magnitude, denoted \(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert,\) is computed by the equation \(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2}.\)

To obtain an angle to describe the direction, we can use our old friend, the tangent function!

Direction Angle of a Vector

For a vector \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle,\) the direction angle \(\theta\) of the vector is the angle formed by a counterclockwise rotation of a ray from the positive \(x\)-axis to the standard position vector. The angle \(\theta\) satisfies the equation \(\tan\theta=\frac{v_2}{v_1}.\)

An easy mental shortcut here is to think of the horizontal component \(v_1\) as the run, the vertical component \(v_2\) as the rise, and the direction as either the slope \(m=\frac{v_2}{v_1}\) (rise over run) or the inclination \(\theta\) (with \(m=\tan\theta\) relating the slope to the inclination).

Component Vector Operations

Scalar Multiplication

Geometrically, the scalar multiple of a vector is a vector that has been stretched and/or reversed by the real number \(k.\) It is easy to compute the components of the scalar multiple of vector \(\overrightarrow{v}\) by the real number \(k\) when we have components:

Scalar Multiplication

Given a vector \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle\) and a real number \(k,\) then \(k\overrightarrow{v}=\langle kv_1,kv_2\rangle.\)

In other words, we "distribute" the scalar \(k\) to the two components.

Vector Addition and Subtraction

Vector addition and subtraction is easier with components, too!

Vector Addition and Subtraction

If \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle\) and \(\overrightarrow{u}=\langle u_1,u_2\rangle,\) then \(\overrightarrow{v}+\overrightarrow{u}=\langle v_1+u_1,v_2+u_2\rangle\) and \(\overrightarrow{v}-\overrightarrow{u}=\langle v_1-u_1,v_2-u_2\rangle.\)

Some Special Vectors

Zero Vector

The vector \(\overrightarrow{0}\) has components \(\langle 0,0\rangle.\) This vector has a magnitude of zero, but it has the unusual feature that it is the only vector that doesn't really have a direction. Alternatively, it could be considered to have every direction, but since the length is zero, it doesn't really matter.

Unit Vectors

Often we want to describe the direction of a vector without using an angle, but still with components. That's where a unit vector comes into play. A vector whose magnitude is \(1\) is called a unit vector. If you divide a vector \(\overrightarrow{v}\) by its length \(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert,\) the result is a unit vector that has the same direction as your original vector \(\overrightarrow{v}.\)

Unit Vector in the Same Direction as \(\overrightarrow{v}\)

The unit vector in the same direction as the nonzero vector \(\overrightarrow{v}\) is \[\frac{\overrightarrow{v}}{\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert}.\]

If \(\theta\) is the direction angle of the vector \(\overrightarrow{v},\) then the components of \(\overrightarrow{v}\) can also be expressed as \(\langle \lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\cos\theta, \lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\sin\theta\rangle.\)

An easy way to think of this is as rectangular vs. polar coordinates. The rectangular components \(v_1\) and \(v_2\) of a vector relate to the "polar coordinates" of a vector \(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\) (playing the role of \(r\)) and \(\theta.\) Via the formulas \(v_1=\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\cos\theta\) (compare to \(x=r\cos\theta\)) and \(v_2=\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\sin\theta\) (compare to \(y=r\sin\theta\)).

Standard Unit Vectors

We define the standard unit vectors \(\overrightarrow{i}\) and \(\overrightarrow{j}\) as follows: \(\overrightarrow{i}=\langle 1,0\rangle\) and \(\overrightarrow{j}=\langle 0,1\rangle.\)

Any vector can be written as a sum of scalar multiples of the standard unit vectors: if \(\overrightarrow{v}=\langle v_1,v_2\rangle,\) then \(\overrightarrow{v}=v_1\overrightarrow{i}+v_2\overrightarrow{j}.\)

Using our discussion about how to use magnitudes, unit vectors, and the standard unit vectors, we can write any vector \(\overrightarrow{v}\) using a combination of its magnitude and its direction angle: \(\overrightarrow{v}=(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\cos\theta)\overrightarrow{i}+(\lVert\overrightarrow{v}\rVert\sin\theta)\overrightarrow{j}.\)

Now we have come full circle. We started by defining vectors as quantities that had both a magnitude and direction. We found that we could work with those quantities more efficiently using components. After developing a lot of notation and sprinkling in our trig knowledge, we can express our vector in terms of its magnitude and direction while keeping the benefits of having components. We'll extend these ideas in the next class.
