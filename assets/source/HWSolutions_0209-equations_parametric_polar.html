  1. Find three points on the graph of the parametric equations \((x,y)=(2\sin t,3\cos t).\)

    When \(t=0\), we get the point \((2,0).\) When \(t=\frac{\pi}{4}\), we get the point \(\left(\sqrt{2},\frac{3\sqrt{2}}{2}\right).\) When \(t=\frac{\pi}{2}\), we get the point \((0,3)\). Other points can be obtained by using other values of \(t.\)

  2. Is the point \((3,4)\) on the graph of the parametric equations \((x,y)=(2t-1,3t+2)\) or not? Explain how you know.

    For the point \((3,4)\) to be on the graph, there must be a time value \(t\) that we can substitute to get this point. Considering the \(x\)-value, such a \(t\)-value must solve the equation \(3=2t-1\). Thus \(t\) must be \(2\). This means that the only way for the point \((3,4)\) to be on the graph is for that to happen when \(t=2\). However, when we use \(t=2\) in the second equation, we get \(y=3(2)+2=8\), which is not the \(y\)-value of \(4\) that we were expecting. Since the only \(t\)-value that makes the \(x\)-value \(3\) makes the \(y\)-value \(6\), the point \((3,4)\) is not on this graph.

  3. Find the \(x\)-intercepts and \(y\)-intercepts, if any, of the graphs of the following parametric equations.

    1. \((x,y)=(2t-1, 3t+2)\)

      When the \(x\)-component \(2t-1\) is zero, we can find the time value(s) of the \(y\)-intercepts. When \(2t-1=0\), we get \(t=\frac{1}{2}\). Substituting \(t=\frac{1}{2}\) into \(3t+2\), we get \(y=\frac{7}{2}\). Thus the only \(y\)-intercept is \(\left(0,\frac{7}{2}\right).\)

      When the \(y\)-component \(3t+2\) is zero, we can find the time value(s) of the \(x\)-intercepts. When \(3t+2=0\), we get \(t=-\frac{2}{3}\). Substituting \(t=-\frac{2}{3}\) into \(2t-1\), we get \(y=-\frac{7}{2}\). Thus the only \(x\)-intercept is \(\left(-\frac{7}{3},0\right).\)

    2. \((x,y)=(t^2-4, 3t+5)\)

      Solving \(t^2-4=0\), we get \(t=\pm 2.\) Thus the \(y\)-intercepts are \(\left(0,11\right)\) at \(t=2\) and \(\left(0,-1\right)\) at \(t=-2.\)

      Solving \(3t+5=0\), we get \(t=-\frac{5}{3}.\) Thus the only \(x\)-intercept is \(\left(-\frac{11}{9},0\right)\) at \(t=-\frac{5}{3}.\)

    3. \((x,y)=(\sqrt{4-t}, \sqrt{t-1})\)

      Solving \(\sqrt{4-t}=0\), we get \(t=4.\) Thus the only \(y\)-intercept is \(\left(0,\sqrt{3}\right)\) at \(t=4.\)

      Solving \(\sqrt{t-1}=0\), we get \(t=1.\) Thus the only \(x\)-intercept is \(\left(\sqrt{3},0\right)\) at \(t=1.\)

    4. \((x,y)=(e^t, \ln(t))\)

      Solving \(e^t=0\) yields no solutions. Thus there are no \(y\)-intercepts.

      Solving \(\ln(t)=0\), we get \(t=1.\) Thus the only \(x\)-intercept is \(\left(e,0\right)\) at \(t=1.\)

    5. \((x,y)=\left(\sin(t), t+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\)

      Solving \(\sin(t)=0\), we get \(t=n\pi\), for all natural numbers \(n.\) Thus there are infinitely many \(y\)-intercepts following the pattern \(\left(0,n\pi+\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\) for all natural numbers \(n.\)

      Solving \(t+\frac{\pi}{2}=0\), we get \(t=-\frac{\pi}{2}.\) Thus the only \(x\)-intercept is \(\left(-1,0\right)\) at \(t=-\frac{\pi}{2}.\)

  4. Find three points on the graph of the polar equation \(r=2-3\cos\theta.\)

    When \(\theta=0^\circ\), we get the point \((-1,0^\circ).\) When \(\theta=90^\circ\), we get the point \((2,90^\circ).\) When \(\theta=180^\circ\), we get the point \((5,180^\circ)\). Other points can be obtained easily by using other values of \(\theta.\)

  5. Is the point \((4,45^\circ)\) on the graph of the polar equation \(r=2\sin\theta+3\cos\theta\) or not? Explain how you know.

    Substitute \(4\) for \(r\) and \(45^\circ\) for \(\theta\). We get \(4=2\sin 45^\circ + 3\cos 45^\circ.\) The right hand side simplifies to \(\frac{5\sqrt{2}}{2}\), which is not equal to \(4\). Thus this point is not on the graph.

  6. Find the \(x\)-intercepts and \(y\)-intercepts, if any, of the graphs of following polar equations.

    1. \(r=2+3\sin\theta\)

      The \(x\)-intercepts occur when \(\theta=0^\circ\) or \(\theta=180^\circ.\) Then \(r=2+3\sin 0^\circ\) tells us that \((2,0^\circ\)) is an \(x\)-intercept and \(r=2+3\sin 180^\circ\) tells us that \((2,180^\circ)\) is also an \(x\)-intercept.

      The \(y\)-intercepts occur when \(\theta=90^\circ\) or \(\theta=270^\circ.\) Then \(r=2+3\sin 90^\circ\) tells us that \((5,90^\circ\)) is a \(y\)-intercept and \(r=2+3\sin 270^\circ\) tells us that \((-1,270^\circ)\) is also a \(y\)-intercept.

      The graph goes through the pole if there is a value of \(\theta\) where \(r=0\). Then \(0=2+3\sin\theta\) tells us that \(\sin\theta=-\frac{2}{3}\); there are infinitely many angles \(\theta\) that work in this equation, including the Quadrant IV angle \(\sin^{-1}\left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)\) (approximately \(-41.81^\circ\)). Thus the graph does go through the pole.

      Thus \((2,0^\circ)\), \((2,180^\circ)\), and the pole are the \(x\)-intercepts and \((5,90^\circ)\), \((-1,270^\circ)\), and the pole are the \(y\)-intercepts.

    2. \(r=\cos(4\theta)\)

      The \(x\)-intercepts occur when \(\theta=0^\circ\) or \(\theta=180^\circ.\) Then \(r=\cos(4\cdot 0^\circ)\) tells us that \((1,0^\circ\)) is an \(x\)-intercept and \(r=\cos(4\cdot 180^\circ)\) tells us that \((1,180^\circ)\) is also an \(x\)-intercept.

      The \(y\)-intercepts occur when \(\theta=90^\circ\) or \(\theta=270^\circ.\) Then \(r=\cos(4\cdot 90^\circ)\) tells us that \((1,90^\circ\)) is a \(y\)-intercept and \(r=\cos(4\cdot 270^\circ)\) tells us that \((1,270^\circ)\) is also a \(y\)-intercept.

      The graph goes through the pole if there is a value of \(\theta\) where \(r=0\). Then we must solve \(0=\cos(4\theta)\). One value that works is exactly \(22.5^\circ.\) Then \(\cos(4\cdot 22.5^\circ)=\cos(90^\circ)=0\), so the graph does go through the pole.

      Thus \((1,0^\circ)\), \((1,180^\circ)\), and the pole are the \(x\)-intercepts and \((1,90^\circ)\), \((1,270^\circ)\), and the pole are the \(y\)-intercepts.
