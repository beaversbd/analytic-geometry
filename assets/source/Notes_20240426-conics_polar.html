To get polar equations for our conic sections, we need to discuss the concept of eccentricity and discover a way to have directrices for hyperbolas and ellipses as well as parabolas.

Eccentricity/Focus/Directrix Definition of the Conics

Earlier in the semester we used two ways to define our nondegenerate conic sections. For hyperbolas and ellipses, we defined them using a fixed sum or difference between a point and the two vertices. However, for parabolas, which have only one focus, we defined its points as having the same distance from the focus as the directrix. It turns out that we can use a focus/directrix definition for ellipses and hyperbolas as well!

First, note that for the parabola, we said that the distance from each point on the graph to the focus is equal to the distance from that point to the directrix. In other words, the ratio of these distances must be exactly \(1\). For the ellipse and hyperbola, we will identify two directrices and fix the ratio of the distances from a point to the a focus and the point to a directrix. This ratio is called the eccentricity \(e\) of the conic section.

Focus/Directrix Definition of the Ellipse

Consider the ellipse centered at the origin with equation \(\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1\) and let \(e=\frac{a}{c}\). It can be proved that this ratio \(e\) is the eccentricity as described above. If we rearrange the equation \(e=\frac{a}{c}\), we get \(c=ae\). Since \(c< a\), it must be the case that \(e< 1\). Since \(a^2=b^2+c^2\), we can also prove that \(b^2=a^2(1-e^2)\). So when \(e=0\), we have that \(a=b\) and the ellipse degenerates to a circle. Thus we go forward with the assumption that \(0< e< 1\) for ellipses.

Let us now define the directrices of an ellipse to be the two lines perpendicular to the major axis at a distance of \(a/e\) from the center of the ellipse. Recall also that \(c=ae\). Thus we get two foci \((\pm ae,0)\) and two directrices \(x=\pm\frac{a}{e}\) for our ellipse with a horizontal major axis.

Focus/Directrix Definition of the Hyperbola

Consider the hyperbola centered at the origin with equation \(\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1\) and let \(e=\frac{a}{c}\). It can be proved that this ratio \(e\) is the eccentricity as described above. If we rearrange the equation \(e=\frac{a}{c}\), we get \(c=ae\). Since \(a< c\), it must be the case that \(e> 1\) for hyperbolas. Since \(c^2=a^2+b^2\), we can also prove that \(b^2=a^2(e^2-1)\).

Let us now define the directrices of a hyperbola to be the two lines perpendicular to the major axis at a distance of \(a/e\) from the center of the ellipse. Recall also that \(c=ae\). Thus we get two foci \((\pm ae,0)\) and two directrices \(x=\pm\frac{a}{e}\) for our hyperbola with a horizontal transverse axis.

Focus/Directrix Definition of the Parabola, Revisited

Earlier we defined the parabola to be the shape where the distance from any point to the focus is equal to the distance from that point to the directrix. Thus the ratio of these distances, is \(1\), which we also take to be the eccentricity. We identified a parameter \(c\) as the distance from the vertex to the focus and to the directrix. For the parabola, \(a=c\) and the vertex also serves as the center. Thus for the parabola, the focus and directrix are at a distance of \(c=ae\) from the vertex/center on the axis of symmetry.

Eccentricities for the Three Nondegenerate Conics

Notice that the eccentricity determines our conic: we have an ellipse when \(0< e< 1\), we have a parabola when \(e=1\) and we have a hyperbola when \(e> 1\). Parabolas only have one directrix, while ellipses and hyperbolas have two.

Deriving the Polar Equations for Conic Sections

To derive an equation for conic sections using polar coordinates, we set a focus to be at the pole \((0,0^\circ)\). First, let's assume our major axis/transverse axis/axis of symmetry is the polar axis \(r=0^\circ\). Thus one of our directrices is a vertical line to the left of the pole; let's say that the distance is \(q\) from the pole to the directrix. For a point \(P=(r,\theta)\) on the conic with eccentricity \(e\), the ratio of the distance from \(P\) to the focus to the distance from \(P\) to the directrix. The distance from \(P\) to the focus is just \(r\) and the distance from \(P\) to the focus is just \(q+r\cos\theta\). Thus \[e=\frac{r}{q+r\cos\theta}.\]

Solving this equation for \(r\) gives us \[r=\frac{eq}{1-e\cos\theta}.\]

What if we wanted a vertical ellipse or hyperbola, or a parabola that opens left, up, or down? In that case, we can adjust our assumptions and derive some other forms. Let's summarize all the options:

Polar Equations for Conic Sections

For a conic section with a focus at the pole, with eccentricity \(e\), and with distance \(q\) from the focus at the pole to the nearest directrix,

  • \(r=\frac{eq}{1-e\cos\theta}\) is the graph of a conic with the directrix perpendicular to the polar axis and to the left of the pole.
  • \(r=\frac{eq}{1+e\cos\theta}\) is the graph of a conic with the directrix perpendicular to the polar axis and to the right of the pole.
  • \(r=\frac{eq}{1-e\sin\theta}\) is the graph of a conic with the directrix parallel to the polar axis and below the pole.
  • \(r=\frac{eq}{1+e\sin\theta}\) is the graph of a conic with the directrix parallel to the polar axis and above the pole.
