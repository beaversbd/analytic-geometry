  1. Convert the following general form equations to standard form. Determine which standard or degenerate conic is given by the equation.

    1. \(y^2-4x-2y+9=0\)

      This equation has only one square term but it has a linear term of the other variable. This should be a parabola.

      \[ \begin{align} y^2-4x-2y+9 &= 0 \\ y^2-2y &= 4x-9 \\ y^2-2y+1 &= 4x-9+1 \\ (y-1)^2 &= 4x-8 \\ (y-1)^2 &= 4(x-2). \end{align} \]

      This is indeed a standard form for a rightward opening parabola.

    2. \(x^2-8x-8y+8=0\)

      This equation has only one square term but it has a linear term of the other variable. This should be a parabola.

      \[ \begin{align} x^2-8x-8y+8 &= 0 \\ x^2-8x &= 8y-8 \\ x^2-8x+16 &= 8y-8+16 \\ (x-4)^2 &= 8y+8 \\ (x-4)^2 &= 8(y+1). \end{align} \]

      This is indeed a standard form for an upward opening parabola.

    3. \(4x^2+y^2+24x-2y+21=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have the same sign. This should be an ellipse or a circle.

      \[ \begin{align} 4x^2+y^2+24x-2y+21 &= 0 \\ (4x^2+24x)+(y^2-2y) &= -21 \\ 4(x^2+6x)+(y^2-2y) &= -21 \\ 4(x^2+6x+9)+(y^2-2y+1) &= -21+4\cdot 9+1 \\ 4(x+3)^2+(y-1)^2 &= 16 \\ \frac{4(x+3)^2}{16}+\frac{(y-1)^2}{16} &= 1 \\ \frac{(x+3)^2}{4}+\frac{(y-1)^2}{16} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for an ellipse with a vertical major axis.

    4. \(x^2+9y^2-10x+36y+52=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have the same sign. This should be an ellipse or a circle.

      \[ \begin{align} x^2+9y^2-10x+36y+52 &= 0 \\ (x^2-10x)+(9y^2+36y) &= -52 \\ (x^2-10x)+9(y^2+4y) &= -52 \\ (x^2-10x+25)+9(y^2+4y+4) &= -52+25+9\cdot 4 \\ (x-5)^2+9(y+2)^2 &= 9 \\ \frac{(x-5)^2}{9}+\frac{9(y+2)^2}{9} &= 1 \\ \frac{(x-5)^2}{9}+\frac{(y+2)^2}{1} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for an ellipse with a horizontal major axis.

    5. \(9x^2-4y^2+90x+32y+125=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have opposite signs. This should be a hyperbola.

      \[ \begin{align} 9x^2-4y^2+90x+32y+125 &= 0\\ (9x^2+90x)+(-4y^2+32y) &= -125 \\ 9(x^2+10x)-4(y^2-8y) &= -125 \\ 9(x^2+10x+25)-4(y^2-8y+16) &= -125+9\cdot 25-4\cdot 16 \\ 9(x+5)^2-4(y-4)^2 &= 36 \\ \frac{9(x+5)^2}{36}-\frac{4(y-4)^2}{36} &= 1 \\ \frac{(x+5)^2}{4}-\frac{(y-4)^2}{9} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for a hyperbola with a horizontal transverse axis.

    6. \(9x^2-16y^2+72x+96y+144=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have opposite signs. This should be a hyperbola.

      \[ \begin{align} 9x^2-16y^2+72x+96y+144 &= 0\\ (9x^2+72x)+(-16y^2+96y) &= -144 \\ 9(x^2+8x)-16(y^2-6y) &= -144 \\ 9(x^2+8x+16)-16(y^2-6y+9) &= -144+9\cdot 16-16\cdot 9 \\ 9(x+4)^2-16(y-3)^2 &= -144 \\ \frac{9(x+4)^2}{-144}-\frac{16(y-3)^2}{-144} &= 1 \\ -\frac{(x+4)^2}{16}+\frac{(y-3)^2}{9} &= 1 \\ \frac{(y-3)^2}{9}-\frac{(x+4)^2}{16} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for a hyperbola with a vertical transverse axis.

    7. \(9x^2+4y^2-72x+16y+160=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have the same sign. This should be an ellipse or a circle.

      \[ \begin{align} 9x^2+4y^2-72x+16y+160 &= 0 \\ (9x^2-72x)+(4y^2+16y) &= -160 \\ 9(x^2-8x)+4(y^2+4y) &= -160 \\ 9(x^2-8x+16)+4(y^2+4y+4) &= -160+9\cdot 16+4\cdot 4 \\ 9(x-4)^2+4(y+2)^2 &= 0. \end{align} \]

      We have failed in transforming this to the standard form for an ellipse or a circle. This is a degenerate case since the right hand side at this stage is zero. The graph of this is just the single point \((4,-2).\)

    8. \(4x^2-y^2-40x+6y+91=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have opposite signs. This should be a hyperbola.

      \[ \begin{align} 4x^2-y^2-40x+6y+91 &= 0 \\ (4x^2-40x)+(-y^2+6y) &= -91 \\ 4(x^2-10x)-(y^2-6y) &= -91 \\ 4(x^2-10x+25)-(y^2-6y+9) &= -91+4\cdot 25-9 \\ 4(x-5)^2-(y-3)^2 &= 0. \end{align} \]

      We have failed in transforming this to the standard form for a hyperbola. This is a degenerate case since the right hand side at this stage is zero.

      \[ \begin{align} 4(x-5)^2-(y-3)^2 &= 0\\ (y-3)^2 &= 4(x-5)^2 \\ y-3 &= \pm 2(x-5). \\ \end{align} \]

      This is the graph of the two intersecting lines \(y-3=4(x-5)\) and \(y-3=-4(x-5).\)

    9. \(4x^2-4x-4y-5=0\)

      This equation has only one square term but it has a linear term of the other variable. This should be a parabola.

      \[ \begin{align} 4x^2-4x-4y-5 &= 0 \\ 4x^2-4x &= 4y+5 \\ 4(x^2-x) &= 4y+5 \\ 4\left(x^2-x+\frac{1}{4}\right) &= 4y+5+4\left(\frac{1}{4}\right) \\ 4\left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &= 4y+6 \\ \left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &=\frac{1}{4}(4)\left(y+\frac{6}{4}\right) \\ \left(x-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &=y+\frac{3}{2}. \end{align} \]

      This is indeed a standard form for an upward opening parabola.

    10. \(16x^2+36y^2+48x-180y+257=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have the same sign. This should be an ellipse or a circle.

      \[ \begin{align} 16x^2+36y^2+48x-180y+257 &= 0 \\ (16x^2+48x)+(36y^2-180y) &= -257 \\ 16(x^2+3x)+36(y^2-5y) &= -257 \\ 16\left(x^2+3x+\frac{9}{4}\right)+36\left(y^2-5y+\frac{25}{4}\right) &= -257+16\cdot\frac{9}{4}+36\cdot\frac{25}{4} \\ 16\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2+36\left(y-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 &= 4 \\ 4\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2+9\left(y-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 &= 1 \\ \frac{\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2}{\frac{1}{4}}+\frac{\left(y-\frac{5}{2}\right)^2}{\frac{1}{9}} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for an ellipse with a horizontal major axis.

    11. \(4x^2-16y^2+12x+16y+69=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have opposite signs. This should be a hyperbola.

      \[ \begin{align} 4x^2-16y^2+12x+16y+69 &= 0 \\ (4x^2+12x)+(-16y^2+16y) &= -69 \\ 4(x^2+3x)-16(y^2-1y) &= -69 \\ 4\left(x^2+3x+\frac{9}{4}\right)-16\left(y^2-1y+\frac{1}{4}\right) &= -69+4\cdot\frac{9}{4}-16\cdot\frac{1}{4} \\ 4\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2-16\left(y-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &= -64 \\ 16\left(y-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2-4\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2 &= 64 \\ \frac{16\left(y-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2}{64}-\frac{4\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2}{64} &= 1 \\ \frac{\left(y-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2}{4}-\frac{\left(x+\frac{3}{2}\right)^2}{16} &= 1. \end{align} \]

      This is a standard form for a hyperbola with a vertical transverse axis.

    12. \(y^2-5y+6=0\)

      There are no \(x\)-variables in this equation, so it is degenerate. Notice that \(y^2-5y+6=(y-3)(y-2),\) and so \(y=3\) or \(y=2.\) This equation is the combined graph of the parallel horizontal lines \(y=3\) and \(y=2.\)

    13. \(25x^2+4y^2-150x+40y+350=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have the same sign. This should be an ellipse or a circle.

      \[ \begin{align} 25x^2+4y^2-150x+40y+350 &= 0 \\ (25x^2-150x)+(4y^2+40y) &= -350 \\ 25(x^2-6x)+4(y^2+10y) &= -350 \\ 25(x^2-6x+9)+4(y^2+10y+25) &= -350+25\cdot 9+4\cdot 25 \\ 25(x-3)^2+4(y+5)^2 &= -25. \end{align} \]

      We have failed in transforming this to the standard form for an ellipse or a circle. This is a degenerate case and there are no points on the graph.

    14. \(4x^2-y^2-2x-y=0\)

      This equation has both square terms and they have opposite signs. This should be a hyperbola.

      \[ \begin{align} 4x^2-y^2-2x-y &= 0\\ (4x^2-2x)+(-y^2-y) &= 0 \\ 4\left(x^2-\frac{1}{2}x\right)-(y^2+y) &= 0 \\ 4\left(x^2-\frac{1}{2}x+\frac{1}{16}\right)-\left(y^2+y+\frac{1}{4}\right) &= 0+4\cdot\frac{1}{16}-\frac{1}{4} \\ 4\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2-\left(y+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &= 0. \end{align} \]

      We have failed in transforming this to the standard form for a hyperbola. This is a degenerate case since the right hand side at this stage is zero.

      \[ \begin{align} 4\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2-\left(y+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &= 0\\ \left(y+\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 &= 4\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)^2 \\ y+\frac{1}{2} &= \pm 2\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right). \\ \end{align} \]

      This is the graph of the two intersecting lines \(y+\frac{1}{2}=2\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right)\) and \(y+\frac{1}{2}=-2\left(x-\frac{1}{4}\right).\)
